# Skill: Email

## Tone

**Nerdy Peer.** Write as someone who works inside the problem every day — knowledgeable, direct, and empathetic to what developers and engineering leaders are actually dealing with. Confident without being promotional. Never corporate, never excited on behalf of the reader.

---

## Style rules

**Casing:** Sentence case everywhere — subject lines, headlines, CTAs. No title case.
> "The future of AI" ✓ — "The Future Of AI" ✗

**Numbers:** Numerals in subject lines and headlines. Spell out one through nine in body prose.

**Perspective:** "You" and "your team." Avoid "we" and "our" unless GitHub collaborated with an external partner or customer — in that case "we" is accurate and appropriate.

**Terminology:**
- *repository* not repo
- *organization* not org
- *open source* (no hyphen)
- *sign in* not log in

**Banned phrases:** paradigm shift, sophisticated solutions, we are excited to announce, unlock, leverage, game-changing, powerful, exciting, seamless, and any phrase that sounds like it belongs in a press release.

**Length:** Body text under 150 words, excluding subject line and preheader. Every sentence must earn its place.

---

## Structure

Every email uses these 9 elements in this exact order.

### 1. Subject line
Short, punchy, sentence case. Creates curiosity or makes a specific claim. Tests well as a declarative ("Your coding agent's failures aren't random") or a question ("Why does your coding agent go sideways?"). Avoid urgency language and superlatives.

### 2. Preheader
One sentence. Expands or adds social proof to the subject line. Appears as preview text in email clients — write it knowing it will be read alongside the subject line, not after it.

### 3. Headline
Sentence case. A direct value statement or imperative. Should advance past the subject line, not repeat it.

### 4. Hero quote
A single, high-impact quote in quotation marks. Sourced from the CTA destination — the article, playbook, case study, or asset the email is linking to. Not from the source interview. No attribution required in the email body; the linked content carries attribution.

### 5. Primary CTA
A short action in brackets. No punctuation inside the brackets.
> `[Learn more]` `[Get the playbook]` `[Read the guide]`

This renders as a designed button (green) in the email template. It appears above the body copy — it is the above-the-fold action for readers who don't scroll.

### 6. The bridge
One to two sentences. Connects the headline and hero quote to the specific value the reader will get. This is where context lives — what the asset is, why it was made, who it's for. Keep it factual and direct.

### 7. The value triad
Exactly three bullet points. This is a system requirement — the template renders it as a visual element. Do not convert to prose, do not reduce to fewer than three bullets. A brief instruction like "no numbered list" refers to numbered lists in prose sections, not this element.

Rules:
- Each starts with a strong action verb (Identify, Reclaim, Increase, Implement, Reduce, Build, etc.)
- Each is a complete sentence ending with a period
- Together they answer: what will the reader learn, what problem does it solve, and what outcome does it produce

Align the bullets to the CTA destination type:
- **Educational (blog, guide, playbook):** what they'll learn, the problem solved, the strategy or framework
- **Proof-oriented (case study, customer story):** hard metrics, time saved, business outcome
- **Product-oriented (product page, feature release):** workflow efficiency, specific capability, friction removed

### 8. The closer
One short sentence. Transitions from the value triad to the final action. Not a summary — a forward motion.

### 9. Final link
A descriptive text link using the asset name, ending with a period. This is the second CTA — it uses language more directly connected to the asset than the primary CTA button above.
> "Read the Engineering System Success playbook."
> "Read the full analysis."

---

## CTA alignment

Reverse-engineer the email from the CTA destination. Before drafting, identify what type of asset the brief is linking to, then:

1. Read the destination to find the hero quote — one line that captures the asset's central claim or a compelling stat
2. Align the value triad to what the asset actually delivers
3. Make sure the subject line and headline tease the specific value of the destination — not a generic version of the topic

The email's job is to make the reader curious enough to click. It does not summarize or preview the asset's content — it creates a gap the asset fills.

## What the skill controls vs. what the brief controls

This skill file contains two types of guidance:

**System requirements — skill wins.** These elements exist because of how the email template is built and rendered in the sending platform. They are structural facts, not editorial preferences. The brief cannot override them.
- The 9-element structure and order
- The value triad (exactly three bullets — the template renders this as a specific visual element; prose is not a valid substitute)
- Sentence case (platform convention)
- One primary CTA button above the fold, one final text link at the bottom

**Editorial defaults — brief wins.** These are best-practice starting points. A brief that specifies otherwise takes precedence.
- Word count
- Tone guidance beyond Nerdy Peer baseline
- Banned phrases
- Subject line angle and form
- Hero quote source and selection
- Bridge length and framing
- CTA text and anchor language
- Closer length and approach
